These notes cover the second lecture, on studying clustering with approximate message passing (AMP), together with its continuation at the start of the third day. The running theme is that clustering a Gaussian mixture can be reframed as a *planted* spin-glass problem, and the cavity method — originally developed to solve the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick (SK) spin glass [@thouless1977solution] — gives both the optimal achievable accuracy and a practical algorithm (AMP) for achieving it. The Gaussian-mixture-clustering analysis follows @lesieur2016phase.

# Unsupervised Learning

The **goal** of unsupervised learning is to find hidden structure in unlabeled data. Three broad families:

- **Dimensionality reduction** (PCA, t-SNE, ...): find a low-dimensional representation that preserves structure.
- **Clustering** (k-means, autoencoders, ...): partition data into groups.
- **Generative models**: learn the data distribution itself.

## Clustering Gaussian Mixtures

The data are $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ for $i = 1,\ldots,m$, drawn from a mixture of $r$ Gaussians:

$$
P(x_i) = \sum_{k=1}^{r} \pi_k\, \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k).
$$

Each point is generated by first picking a cluster $k$ with probability $\pi_k$, then sampling from that cluster's Gaussian. The three central questions are:

- **Information-theoretic threshold.** When is it *possible in principle* to recover the cluster memberships from the data — i.e., does the posterior distribution over memberships concentrate, even if finding it requires unbounded computational time?
- **Computational threshold.** When does a polynomial-time algorithm exist that achieves this?
- **Easy vs. hard.** Do these two thresholds coincide, or is there a *statistical-to-computational gap* — a regime where reconstruction is possible in principle but no known efficient algorithm succeeds?

This three-way comparison is the recurring structural question across statistical-physics approaches to inference problems: physics tools (replica method, cavity method) compute the information-theoretic threshold, while message-passing algorithms probe what is achievable efficiently.

# Setting Up the Model

Throughout, take the high-dimensional limit $n, m \to \infty$ with the number of clusters $r = \mathcal{O}(1)$ fixed and the **sample complexity** $\alpha = m/n = \mathcal{O}(1)$ fixed.

## Step 1: The Generative Model

Following @lesieur2016phase, each data point is generated as

$$
x_i = \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{n}}\, V_{t_i} + u_i,
$$

where:

- $t_i \sim \text{Uniform}[1, r]$ is the (hidden) cluster assignment of point $i$, with $\pi_k = 1/r$ for all $k$ (balanced clusters);
- $V_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_n)$ for $k = 1,\ldots,r$ are the (random, unknown) cluster centroids;
- $u_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_n)$ is isotropic noise;
- $\rho$ is the **signal-to-noise ratio parameter**: larger $\rho$ means the centroids are more separated relative to the noise, making clustering easier.

**Physical intuition.** This is structurally the same kind of "signal plus noise" decomposition used in the binary Gaussian mixture of Lecture 1, but now there are $r$ possible centroids instead of $2$, and we must additionally infer the discrete label $t_i$ together with the continuous centroids $V_k$. This makes it a **joint estimation problem**: the unknowns are both a continuous low-rank object ($V$) and a discrete combinatorial object (the assignments).

## Step 2: Reformulation as Low-Rank Matrix Factorization

It is convenient to package the unknowns into matrices. Define the centroid matrix

$$
V = \big(V_1 \;\; V_2 \;\; \cdots \;\; V_r\big) \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times r},
$$

and represent each assignment $t_i$ as a one-hot row vector. Concretely, $\underline{s}_i \in \{e_k\}_{k=1}^r$, where $e_k = (0,\ldots,0,1,0,\ldots,0)$ has a $1$ in position $k$ (the $k$-th standard basis vector). Stacking these rows gives the assignment matrix

$$
S = \begin{pmatrix} \text{---}\, \underline{s}_1 \,\text{---} \\ \text{---}\, \underline{s}_2 \,\text{---} \\ \vdots \\ \text{---}\, \underline{s}_m \,\text{---} \end{pmatrix} \in \{0,1\}^{m\times r}, \qquad S_{ik} = \delta_{t_i, k}.
$$

The full data matrix, with $U = (u_1\;\cdots\;u_m) \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$, is then a **low-rank-plus-noise** decomposition:

$$
X = (x_1\;\cdots\;x_m) = \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{n}}\, \underbrace{V S^\top}_{\text{rank } r} + U \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}.
$$

This is exactly a **low-rank, asymmetric matrix factorization** problem: recover $V$ (which will give the centroids) and $S$ (which will give the assignments) from a noisy observation of their product. Casting clustering this way connects it to the broader family of low-rank matrix estimation problems (PCA, matrix completion, community detection) studied with the same toolkit.

## Step 3: The Bayesian Approach

Treating $V$ and $S$ as the unknowns and $X$ as the observation, Bayes' rule gives the joint distribution

$$
P(X, V, S) = P(X \mid V, S)\, P(V, S) = P(X \mid V, S)\, P(V)\, P(S),
$$

assuming independent priors on $V$ and $S$. Explicitly,

$$
P(X, V, S) = \left(\prod_{i=1}^n P_V(\underline{v}_i)\right)\left(\prod_{j=1}^m P_S(\underline{s}_j)\right)\left(\prod_{i,j} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\Big(X_{ij} - \sqrt{\tfrac{\rho}{n}}\,\underline{v}_i^\top \underline{s}_j\Big)^2\right)\right),
$$

where the rows of $V$ are $\underline{v}_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_r)$, so

$$
P_V(\underline{v}_i) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{r/2}} e^{-\|\underline{v}_i\|^2/2},
$$

and each row of $S$ is uniform over the one-hot vectors,

$$
P_S(\underline{s}_j) = \frac{1}{r}\sum_{k=1}^r \delta_{\underline{s}_j, e_k}.
$$

*(Note: the original handwritten notes wrote this prior as $\delta_{\underline{s}_k}$, which is not well-defined since $\underline{s}_j$ ranges over vectors, not indices; the corrected expression places the Kronecker delta between the random row $\underline{s}_j$ and each candidate basis vector $e_k$.)*

The posterior is obtained by conditioning on the data:

$$
P(S, V \mid X) = \frac{1}{P(X)} P(X, V, S) = \frac{1}{\mathcal{Z}(X)}\prod_i P_V(\underline{v}_i) \prod_j P_S(\underline{s}_j)\; e^{\mathcal{H}(S,V)},
$$

where $\mathcal{H}(S, V)$ — the **Hamiltonian** — collects the data-dependent (likelihood) terms from the Gaussian channel above, and $\mathcal{Z}(X)$ is the partition function (normalization). This is the same Gibbs-measure structure as in Lecture 1: the posterior over the hidden variables is a Boltzmann distribution at $\beta = 1$ whose energy is (minus) the log-likelihood of the data.

# Nishimori Identities

Before tackling the clustering problem directly, it is useful to establish a set of exact identities that hold whenever the statistical model is correctly specified. These are the **Nishimori identities**, a cornerstone tool in the statistical physics of Bayesian inference, used repeatedly below to simplify the cavity calculation.

## What "Bayes-Optimal" Means Here

In any Bayesian inference problem there are, in principle, *two* generative models in play:

- The **true** one, which actually produced the data: a ground-truth signal $x^*$ drawn from some true prior $P_X^*$, passed through a true noisy channel to produce the observation, $y \sim P_{Y\mid X^*}^*$.
- The **assumed** one, which the "student" (the statistician, or the inference algorithm) uses to build the posterior: an assumed prior $P_X$ and an assumed channel $P_{Y\mid X}$, combined via Bayes' rule into $P_{X\mid Y}(x) \propto P_X(x)\, P_{Y\mid X}(y)$.

These two models need not agree. If your data were generated by one mechanism but you (perhaps unknowingly) build your posterior using the *wrong* prior or the *wrong* noise model, your inference is **misspecified**, and there is generally no clean relationship between properties of the posterior and properties of the true signal.

**Bayes-optimality** is the special case where the student's assumed model is *exactly* the true one:

$$
P_X = P_X^*, \qquad P_{Y\mid X} = P_{Y\mid X}^*.
$$

This is not a statement about the student doing a particularly good job algorithmically — it is a statement that the student's *model is correct*. In the clustering problem above, for instance, this means the student knows the true number of clusters $r$, the true noise level (encoded in $\rho$ or $\lambda$), and the true cluster-generation mechanism, and builds the posterior $P(V, S \mid X)$ using exactly these. This is, admittedly, a somewhat idealized setting — in practice one rarely knows the true model exactly — but it is the setting in which the cleanest theoretical statements can be made, and it gives the *information-theoretic benchmark* against which any real algorithm (which may not know the true model) can be compared.

## Setup and Derivation

Suppose, as above, $x^* \sim P_X^*$ and $y \sim P_{Y\mid X^*}^*$. A student who has built a posterior $P_{X\mid Y}$ wants to evaluate the expectation of some test function $f_y(x)$ of the unknown variable — for concreteness, keep in mind the running example $f_y(x) = x$, so that $\mathbb{E}_{x\mid y}[f_y(x)]$ is just the posterior mean (the MMSE estimator from the previous section).

There are two natural quantities one might consider:

1. **Evaluate $f_y$ at the true signal.** Average $f_y(x^*)$ over the true randomness in both $x^*$ and $y$:
   $$
   \mathbb{E}_{y,x^*}\big[f_y(x^*)\big] = \mathbb{E}_{x^*}\,\mathbb{E}_{y\mid x^*}\big[f_y(x^*)\big] = \int dx^*\,dy\; P_X^*(x^*)\, P_{Y\mid X^*}^*(y)\, f_y(x^*).
   $$
   Since $x^*$ is just a dummy variable of integration drawn from $P_X^*$, we can relabel it $x^*\to x$ without changing the value of the integral:
   $$
   \mathbb{E}_{y,x^*}\big[f_y(x^*)\big] = \int dx\, dy\; P_X^*(x)\, P_{Y\mid X}^*(y)\, f_y(x). \tag{A}
   $$

2. **Evaluate $f_y$ at a posterior sample.** Draw $x$ from the posterior $P_{X\mid Y}$ given $y$, then average over the marginal law of $y$:
   $$
   \mathbb{E}_{y,x}\big[f_y(x)\big] = \mathbb{E}_y\,\mathbb{E}_{x\mid y}\big[f_y(x)\big] = \int dx\, dy\; P_{X\mid Y}(x)\, P_Y(y)\, f_y(x).
   $$
   By Bayes' rule, $P_{X\mid Y}(x)\,P_Y(y) = P_{Y\mid X}(y)\,P_X(x)$ (this is just the joint density of $(x,y)$ under the *assumed* model, written two different ways), so
   $$
   \mathbb{E}_{y,x}\big[f_y(x)\big] = \int dx\, dy\; P_{Y\mid X}(y)\, P_X(x)\, f_y(x). \tag{B}
   $$

**Comparing (A) and (B).** Expression (A) involves the *true* prior $P_X^*$ and *true* channel $P_{Y\mid X}^*$; expression (B) involves the assumed prior $P_X$ and assumed channel $P_{Y\mid X}$. These are, in general, completely different integrals — there is no reason for the true-data average in (A) to equal the posterior-sample average in (B) for a misspecified model. **This is exactly where Bayes-optimality enters**: if and only if $P_X = P_X^*$ and $P_{Y\mid X} = P_{Y\mid X}^*$, the integrands in (A) and (B) are identical term-by-term, and we obtain the **Nishimori identity**:

$$
\boxed{\mathbb{E}_{x^*,y}\big[f_y(x^*)\big] = \mathbb{E}_{x,y}\big[f_y(x)\big] \qquad \text{(Bayes-optimal setting only).}}
$$

## Physical and Practical Meaning

In words: under Bayes-optimality, a sample $x$ drawn from the posterior is **statistically indistinguishable** from the ground truth $x^*$ itself, in the sense that any function of $x$ has the same average as the same function of $x^*$, provided we also average over the data-generating randomness $y$. This holds even though, for any *single* dataset $y$, the posterior sample $x$ and the true $x^*$ are generally not equal — the identity is a statement about matching distributions, not about pointwise agreement.

A useful way to build intuition: think of generating many problem instances. Each time, draw a fresh $x^* \sim P_X^*$, generate $y$ from it, and then draw a posterior sample $x \sim P_{X\mid Y}(\cdot\mid y)$ using the *correct* model. Because the model is correct, the pair $(x^*, y)$ and the pair $(x, y)$ are generated by literally the same joint law $P_X(x)P_{Y\mid X}(y)$ — there is simply no statistical way to tell, from the joint distribution alone, which of $x^*$ or $x$ played the role of "ground truth" and which played the role of "posterior sample." They are exchangeable.

**Why this matters for the calculations below.** The Nishimori identity is the single most useful technical shortcut in replica and cavity calculations for Bayes-optimal inference, because it lets us freely substitute the planted ground truth $x^*$ for an independent posterior sample (or "replica") inside any expectation, and vice versa. Concretely, in the cavity calculation later in these notes we will define the two order parameters
$$
m = \mathbb{E}\big[\langle x_0\, x_0^*\rangle\big] \quad\text{(overlap between a posterior sample and the truth)}, \qquad q = \mathbb{E}\big[\langle x_0\rangle^2\big] \quad\text{(overlap between two independent posterior samples)}.
$$
Without the Nishimori identity, $m$ and $q$ would be two genuinely different quantities requiring separate equations. But applying the identity with $f_y(x) = x\,\langle x_0\rangle$ (replacing $x^*_0$, which appears in $m$, by an independent posterior sample $\langle x_0 \rangle$, since the two are exchangeable under Bayes-optimality) gives directly
$$
m = q \qquad \text{(Bayes-optimal setting)},
$$
collapsing two order parameters into one and substantially simplifying the self-consistency equations. This is exactly the simplification used below when we reduce the cavity recursion to a single state-evolution variable.

# Observables

To quantify reconstruction quality, define the per-cluster squared error between an estimate $\hat{\underline{v}}$ and the truth $\underline{v}^*$:

$$
\text{SE}(\hat{\underline{v}}, \underline{v}^*) = \frac{1}{r}\sum_k \big(\hat{v}_k - v_k^*\big)^2,
$$

and the mean squared error obtained by averaging over the posterior $\mu(\underline{v} \mid x)$ of a single row given the data $x$:

$$
\text{MSE}(\hat{\underline{v}} \mid x) = \int d\underline{v}\; \mu(\underline{v}\mid x)\,\text{SE}(\hat{\underline{v}}, \underline{v}),
$$

where the marginal posterior of one row is obtained by integrating out all the others,

$$
\mu(\underline{v}_i \mid X) = \int \left(\prod_{i'\neq i} d\underline{v}_{i'} \prod_j d\underline{s}_j\right) P(V, S \mid X).
$$

The **minimum mean squared error (MMSE) estimator** is the posterior mean,

$$
\hat{\underline{v}} = \int d\underline{v}\; \underline{v}\,\mu(\underline{v}\mid X),
$$

which is the Bayes-optimal point estimate under squared-error loss — a standard fact from decision theory, here specialized to this inference problem.

For the discrete assignments, the natural notion of overlap is whether the estimated label matches the truth:

$$
O(\hat{\underline{s}}, \underline{s}^*) = \delta_{\hat{\underline{s}}, \underline{s}^*} \quad (\text{overlap}),
$$

with mean overlap $\text{MO}(\hat{\underline{s}}) = \mu(\hat{\underline{s}} \mid \underline{s}^*)$, and the Bayes-optimal (maximum-overlap) estimator is obtained by picking, for each point, the cluster with highest posterior probability:

$$
\hat{\underline{s}}^{\,\text{MMO}} = \arg\max_{k} \mu(e_k \mid X).
$$

# From Clustering to the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick Model

The key trick that makes this problem solvable with cavity/spin-glass methods is to **symmetrize** the rectangular factorization into a square, symmetric one. Recall

$$
X = \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{n}}\, VS^\top + U \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}.
$$

For the special case $r = 1$ (just two symmetric clusters, or more precisely a single rank-one direction), one can instead consider directly the symmetric (square) observation

$$
X \;\rightsquigarrow\; X = \sqrt{\frac{\rho}{n}}\, VV^\top + U,
$$

which is an instance of the canonical **spiked Wigner model**:

$$
Y = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\, \underline{x}\,\underline{x}^\top + \xi, \qquad \underline{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times 1},\; \xi \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n},
$$

where $\xi_{ij} = \xi_{ji} \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is symmetric Gaussian noise (a Gaussian orthogonal ensemble, "Wigner" matrix) and $\underline{x} \sim P_X(\underline{x}) = \prod_i P_X(x_i)$ is the planted signal. This is the simplest possible rank-one symmetric matrix estimation problem, and it underlies the rank-$r$ clustering problem in the same way that the binary perceptron underlies general classification: it is the minimal model that captures the essential physics.

**Posterior.** By Bayes' rule, exactly as before,

$$
P(\underline{x} \mid Y) = \frac{1}{P(Y)} \prod_i P_X(x_i) \prod_{i\le j}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\Big(Y_{ij} - \sqrt{\tfrac{\lambda}{n}}\,x_i x_j\Big)^2\right)\right) = \frac{1}{\mathcal{Z}(Y)}\prod_i P_X(x_i)\, e^{\mathcal{H}(\underline{x})}.
$$

Expanding the square and dropping terms independent of $\underline{x}$ (absorbed into the normalization), the Hamiltonian is

$$
\mathcal{H}(\underline{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}} \sum_{i\le j} Y_{ij}\, x_i x_j \;-\; \frac{\lambda}{2n}\sum_{i \le j} x_i^2 x_j^2.
$$

**Connection to the SK model.** If the prior is the Ising (Rademacher) distribution, $P_X(x_i) = \tfrac12 \delta_{x_i,+1} + \tfrac12\delta_{x_i,-1}$, then $x_i^2 = 1$ for every spin, and the second term above becomes an irrelevant constant. The Hamiltonian reduces to

$$
\mathcal{H}(\underline{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\sum_{i<j} Y_{ij}\, x_i x_j,
$$

which is precisely the **Sherrington–Kirkpatrick (SK) Hamiltonian** for an Ising spin glass with random couplings $J_{ij} \propto Y_{ij}$. The SK model — for which the cavity method and replica method were originally developed, and whose solution by Parisi earned the 2021 Nobel Prize in Physics — is normally studied with $Y_{ij}$ as *pure, unstructured* random disorder. Here, instead, $Y_{ij}$ contains a hidden low-rank "plant" $\sqrt{\lambda/n}\,x_i^*x_j^*$ buried in Gaussian noise — this is the **planted SK model**, and the question of how well one can recover $\underline{x}^*$ from $Y$ is the spin-glass analogue of the clustering problem above.

## The Cavity Method for the Planted SK Model

We now solve the planted SK model using the **cavity method** [@mezard1987spin], following the original logic of TAP [@thouless1977solution]: rather than computing the full partition function with replicas, build up the system one spin at a time and track how adding a spin perturbs the existing equilibrium.

Write the Hamiltonian for a system of $n$ spins as

$$
\mathcal{H}_n = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\le j}^n \Big(Y_{ij} - \sqrt{\tfrac{\lambda}{n}}\,x_i x_j\Big)^2.
$$

Substituting the planted model $Y_{ij} = \sqrt{\lambda/n}\,x_i^* x_j^* + \xi_{ij}$ and expanding the square,

$$
\mathcal{H}_n = \text{const.} - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\le j}^n\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}x_i^2 x_j^2 - \frac{2\lambda}{n}x_i x_j x_i^* x_j^* - 2\sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\,\xi_{ij}\,x_i x_j\right).
$$

**Adding one spin.** The cavity method's central move: imagine adding a single new spin $x_0$ to a system that already has $n$ spins in equilibrium, giving a system of $n+1$ spins:

$$
\mathcal{H}_{n+1} = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i\le j}^{n}\Big(\cdots\Big) \;-\; \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\left(\frac{\lambda}{n+1}x_0^2 x_j^2 - \frac{2\lambda}{n+1}x_0 x_j x_0^* x_j^* - 2\sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n+1}}\,\xi_{0j}\,x_0 x_j\right).
$$

The first sum is exactly $\mathcal{H}_n(\lambda')$ with a rescaled coupling $\lambda' = \lambda n/(n+1)$ — i.e. to leading order in $1/n$ it is just the old Hamiltonian on the original $n$ spins. The remaining terms are the new spin's interaction with the rest of the system — its **cavity field**.

**Ensemble average.** Define the thermal average over $n$ spins (with the planted spins $x_i^*$ also random, drawn from $P_X$, but held fixed when computing the average — i.e. *quenched*):

$$
\langle(\cdot)\rangle_n \equiv \frac{\int dx_1\cdots dx_n\; P_X(x_1)\cdots P_X(x_n)\;(\cdot)\; e^{\mathcal{H}_n(\underline{x})}}{\int dx_1 \cdots dx_n\; P_X(x_1)\cdots P_X(x_n)\; e^{\mathcal{H}_n(\underline{x})}}.
$$

Then the average over the $(n+1)$-spin system can be related to the $n$-spin average by isolating the new spin's marginal:

$$
\langle(\cdot)\rangle_{n+1} = \left\langle \frac{\displaystyle\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\,(\cdot)\,\exp\!\left(\sqrt{\tfrac{\lambda}{n}}\,x_0\sum_i \xi_{0i}\,x_i + \tfrac{\lambda}{n}x_0 x_i^*\, x_i^* \cdots - \tfrac{\lambda x_0^2}{2n}\sum_i x_i^2\right)}{\langle(\,\cdot\,)\rangle}\right\rangle_n.
$$

**Physics assumption: concentration.** The key physical input — justified rigorously in many cases, and the working assumption throughout the cavity method — is that empirical sums over many spins *concentrate* around their expectations as $n\to\infty$ (a law-of-large-numbers statement). This lets us define three macroscopic **order parameters**:

$$
m = \mathbb{E}\big[\langle x_0\, x_0^*\rangle_n\big] \quad \text{(magnetization)}, \qquad \frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i x_i^* \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{} m,
$$

$$
\rho = \mathbb{E}\big[\langle x_0^2\rangle_n\big], \qquad \frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i^2 \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{} \rho, \qquad q = \mathbb{E}\big[\langle x_0\rangle_n^2\big],
$$

and self-consistency as $n\to\infty$ requires $\mathbb{E}[\langle x_0 x_0^*\rangle_{n+1}] = m$ as well — adding one more spin does not change the macroscopic state.

**Simplifying the cavity field.** Using concentration, the sum $\sum_i \xi_{0i}\, x_i$ appearing in the new spin's effective field is a sum of $n$ independent, weakly-correlated terms, so by the central limit theorem it converges to a Gaussian:

$$
\sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\sum_i \xi_{0i}\, x_i \;\rightsquigarrow\; \sqrt{\lambda q}\; z, \qquad z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1).
$$

This Gaussian-fluctuation argument — replacing a sum of many weakly-dependent contributions by a single effective Gaussian field — is the cavity method's analogue of the central limit theorem underlying mean-field theory, and is the same mechanism that makes AMP's "state evolution" exact in the high-dimensional limit.

Putting the pieces together, the self-consistent equation for the magnetization becomes

$$
m = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, x_0\, x_0^*\, \exp\!\left(\lambda x_0 x_0^* m - \frac{\lambda \rho}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q}\, z\, x_0\right)}{\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\,\exp\!\left(\lambda x_0 x_0^* m - \frac{\lambda \rho}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q}\,z\,x_0\right)}\right].
$$

**Bayes-optimal simplification.** In the Bayes-optimal setting, the Nishimori identity gives $m = q$ (as derived in the Nishimori Identities section above, where $m$ plays the role of the truth–sample overlap and $q$ the role of the sample–sample overlap). Iterating this self-consistency equation from the trivial fixed point $m^0 = 0$ gives the **state evolution equations**:

$$
m^{t+1} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, x_0\, x_0^*\, \exp\!\left(\lambda x_0 x_0^* m^t - \frac{\lambda \rho^t}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q^t}\,z\, x_0\right)}{\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, \exp\!\left(\lambda x_0 x_0^* m^t - \frac{\lambda \rho^t}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q^t}\,z\, x_0\right)}\right].
$$

Iterating these equations to a fixed point gives the asymptotic MMSE achievable by Bayes-optimal inference — exactly mirroring the saddle-point equations of the replica calculation in Lecture 1, but derived by a different (cavity) route.

# From the Cavity Method to AMP

The cavity recursion above, written for a *generic* (not necessarily Ising) prior $P_X$, can be expressed compactly using a **denoiser function**. Define

$$
\eta(A, B) = \frac{\int dx\, P_X(x)\, \exp\!\left(-\frac{A}{2}x^2 + Bx\right)}{\int dx\, P_X(x)\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{A}{2}x^2 + Bx\right)},
$$

which is exactly the posterior mean of $x$ under a Gaussian observation model with prior $P_X$, precision $A$, and "evidence" $B$ — i.e. $\eta(A,B)$ is the Bayes-optimal denoiser for a single scalar variable observed through additive Gaussian noise. (This is the same object as the "MMSE estimator" used to define the observables above, here applied to one coordinate at a time.)

In terms of $\eta$, the recursive update for the cavity mean becomes

$$
\langle x_0\rangle_{n+1} = \eta\!\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\sum_i \langle x_i^2\rangle_n,\; \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\sum_i Y_{0i}\langle x_i\rangle_n\right).
$$

**TAP equations.** Generalizing this from "add one spin to $n$" to "the marginal of spin $i$ given all others" yields the **TAP equations** [@thouless1977solution]:

$$
\langle x_i \rangle_n = \eta\!\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\sum_{j\neq i}\langle x_j^2\rangle_n,\; \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\sum_{j\neq i} Y_{ij}\langle x_j\rangle_n\right).
$$

Since dropping a single index $i$ from a sum over $n$ terms is a relative $\mathcal{O}(1/n)$ correction, we can extend the sums to run over all indices (including the "self" term, which only contributes at subleading order):

$$
\langle x_i\rangle_n \approx \eta\!\left(\frac{\lambda}{n+1}\sum_{j=0}^n \langle x_j^2\rangle_{n+1} + \mathcal{O}(1/n),\; \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\sum_j Y_{ij}\langle x_j\rangle_{n+1}\right).
$$

**The Onsager correction term.** Naively iterating the equation $\langle x_i\rangle_{n+1} = \eta(\cdots)$ using all spins' *current* averages double-counts spin $i$'s own contribution to the field acting on itself (since spin $i$ influences its neighbors, which then influence it back). A first-order Taylor expansion of $\eta$ in the small parameter that distinguishes the "leave-one-out" sum from the full sum produces a correction term:

$$
\langle x_i\rangle_{n+1} \approx \underbrace{\eta\!\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\sum_j \langle x_j^2\rangle_{n+1},\; \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\sum_j Y_{ij}\langle x_j\rangle_{n+1}\right)}_{\text{naive update}} \;-\; \underbrace{\sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\,\eta'\, Y_{i0}\langle x_0\rangle_{n+1}}_{\text{Onsager term}},
$$

where $\eta'$ denotes the derivative of $\eta$ with respect to its second argument. This **Onsager reaction term** — first identified in the TAP solution of the SK model [@thouless1977solution] — is essential: without it, the naive mean-field iteration does not converge to the correct fixed point, because it ignores the self-consistent "reaction" of a spin's own past influence propagating back to it through its neighbors.

**The AMP algorithm.** Collecting these corrections into a vectorized update over time steps $t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ gives the **Approximate Message Passing (AMP)** algorithm:

$$
\begin{cases}
\underline{h}^{t} = \sqrt{\dfrac{\lambda}{n}}\, Y\, \underline{x}^{t} \;-\; \lambda\!\left(\dfrac{1}{n}\sum_i \eta_i'\right)\underline{x}^{t-1} & \text{(cavity field, with Onsager correction)} \\[2mm]
\underline{x}^{t+1} = \eta\!\left(\dfrac{\lambda}{n}\underline{x}^{t}\cdot\underline{x}^{t},\; \underline{h}^{t}\right) & \text{(denoising step)}
\end{cases}
$$

AMP is an iterative algorithm that alternates between computing an effective field $\underline{h}^t$ (matrix-vector multiplication, corrected by the Onsager term to remove unwanted feedback) and denoising it coordinate-wise with $\eta$. Crucially, its trajectory is described *exactly*, in the $n\to\infty$ limit, by the same scalar **state evolution** recursion derived above via the cavity method:

$$
\underline{h}^t = \lambda\, m^t\, \underline{x}_0^* + \sqrt{\lambda q^t}\; \underline{z},
$$

i.e. the empirical effect of the matrix-vector product on a true signal coordinate is, asymptotically, indistinguishable from a single scalar Gaussian channel with signal-to-noise set by $(m^t, q^t)$. This is the AMP/cavity-method version of the same phenomenon that drove the replica calculation in Lecture 1: a high-dimensional problem collapses onto a low-dimensional (here, scalar) effective description.

This rank-one AMP recipe generalizes directly to the rank-$r$, asymmetric clustering problem with which we started — replacing scalars $(x_i, m, q)$ by length-$r$ vectors and matrices — giving the **low-rank AMP algorithm for clustering Gaussian mixtures** developed in @lesieur2016phase, which both achieves the Bayes-optimal MMSE (matching the information-theoretic threshold, when there is no statistical-to-computational gap) and runs in time linear in the size of the data per iteration.

# The Bethe Free Energy

*(Day 3, continuing directly from the cavity calculation above.)*

The cavity method gives the state evolution equations governing $(m^t, q^t)$, but it is also useful to have a single scalar quantity — a **free energy** — whose extremization directly reproduces these equations, in the same spirit as the replica free energy of Lecture 1. This is the **Bethe free energy**, and deriving it gives an independent check on the cavity calculation as well as a variational characterization of the fixed point.

## Setup

Recall the recursion for the Hamiltonian when adding spin $x_0$ to a system of $n$ spins, written out to the order needed below:

$$
\mathcal{H}_{n+1} = \mathcal{H}_n(\lambda') + \sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\,x_0\sum_i \xi_{0i}\, x_i + \frac{\lambda}{n}\,x_0 x_0^*\sum_i x_i x_i^* - \frac{\lambda x_0^2}{2n}\sum_i x_i^2 + \mathcal{O}_n(1),
$$

where, as before, $\lambda' = \lambda n/(n+1)$ is the rescaled coupling for the $n$-spin sub-system. Define the **free energy density**

$$
\phi_B = \lim_{n\to\infty} \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\log Z_n\right], \qquad Z_n = \int d\underline{x}\; P_X(\underline{x})\, \exp\!\big(\mathcal{H}_n(\underline{x})\big).
$$

**Telescoping trick.** Rather than tackle $\log Z_n$ directly, write it as a telescoping (Cesàro) sum of single-spin increments:

$$
\frac{1}{n}\log Z_n = \frac{1}{n}\log\!\left(\frac{Z_n}{Z_{n-1}}\cdot\frac{Z_{n-1}}{Z_{n-2}}\cdots\frac{Z_1}{Z_0}\right) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\log\!\left(\frac{Z_i}{Z_{i-1}}\right).
$$

Since each individual ratio $Z_i/Z_{i-1}$ has (in expectation, by the self-averaging/concentration property used throughout the cavity method) the *same* limiting value as $n\to\infty$ — adding the $i$-th spin to an already-large system looks statistically the same regardless of $i$ — the Cesàro mean of these increments converges to that common limiting value:

$$
\phi_B = \lim_{n\to\infty}\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log\frac{Z_n}{Z_{n-1}}\right].
$$

This reduces the free energy density to the expected log of a *single* cavity step — exactly the ratio $Z_{n+1}/Z_n$ computed via the recursion above (with $n-1 \to n$ relabeled to match the "adding $x_0$" notation used earlier).

## Evaluating the Cavity Ratio

From the definition of $Z_n$ and the Hamiltonian recursion,

$$
\frac{Z_{n+1}}{Z_n} = \left\langle \exp\!\left(\big[\mathcal{H}_n(\lambda') - \mathcal{H}_n\big]\right) \int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, \exp\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{\lambda}{n}}\,x_0\sum_i \xi_{0i}x_i + \frac{\lambda}{n}x_0 x_0^*\sum_i x_i x_i^* - \frac{\lambda x_0^2}{2n}\sum_i x_i^2\right)\right\rangle_n.
$$

The bracketed rescaling factor $\mathcal{H}_n(\lambda') - \mathcal{H}_n$ contributes only at subleading order and can be dropped in the $n\to\infty$ limit (it is the same $\mathcal{O}(1/n)$ correction encountered when deriving the TAP equations). Using the concentration results from before — $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i x_i^* \to m$, $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i^2 \to \rho$ — and the Gaussian central-limit approximation $\sqrt{\lambda/n}\sum_i \xi_{0i}x_i \rightsquigarrow \sqrt{\lambda q}\,z$ derived earlier, the $x_0$-integral collapses to a single scalar object:

$$
\log\frac{Z_{n+1}}{Z_n} \;\longrightarrow\; \log\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, \exp\!\left(\lambda m\, x_0 x_0^* - \frac{\lambda\rho}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q}\, x_0\, z\right).
$$

This is exactly the same integral that appears inside the state-evolution update derived earlier — reassuringly, since the free energy and the cavity recursion describe the same underlying fixed point.

## The Disorder-Average Correction via Stein's Lemma

There is, however, one more piece: the average $\langle(\cdot)\rangle_n$ on the outside is itself still a thermal average over the $n$-spin system, and this average implicitly depends on the *same* disorder $\xi_{ij}$ that entered $\mathcal{H}_n$ in the first place. Separating out this residual dependence requires controlling a term of the form

$$
\prod_{i,j}\mathbb{E}_\xi\!\left[\exp\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt{\lambda}}{4n\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i,j}\xi_{ij}\,\langle x_i x_j\rangle_n\right)\right],
$$

i.e. the disorder average of an exponential whose exponent is *linear* in the Gaussian noise $\xi_{ij}$, but whose coefficient $\langle x_i x_j\rangle_n$ also depends on $\xi_{ij}$ through the Gibbs average. This self-referential dependence is handled using **Stein's lemma**, which states that for $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and any suitably smooth function $g$,

$$
\mathbb{E}_z\big[z\, g(z)\big] = \mathbb{E}_z\big[g'(z)\big].
$$

Applying this (in its multivariate form, coordinate-wise in each $\xi_{ij}$) to differentiate the Gibbs average with respect to its own disorder gives

$$
\mathbb{E}\big[\xi_{ij}\,\langle x_i x_j\rangle_n\big] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\partial \langle x_i x_j\rangle_n}{\partial \xi_{ij}}\right] = \mathbb{E}\Big[\langle x_i^2 x_j^2\rangle_n - \langle x_i x_j\rangle_n^2\Big],
$$

where the right-hand side is a standard fluctuation–response identity: differentiating a Gibbs average with respect to a parameter coupled linearly to an observable produces a variance-like term (the connected correlation, here between $x_i x_j$ and itself). This is the same type of identity that produces susceptibilities and response functions throughout statistical mechanics — it is the discrete/disordered analogue of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem.

Carrying this correction through the calculation and using concentration once more (so that $\langle x_i^2 x_j^2\rangle_n \to \rho^2$ and $\langle x_i x_j\rangle_n^2 \to q^2$), the disorder-average factor evaluates to

$$
\exp\!\left(-\frac{\lambda}{4}\rho^2 + \frac{\lambda}{4}q^2\right).
$$

## The Bethe Free Energy

Collecting all the pieces — the scalar cavity integral and the disorder-average correction — gives the final closed-form expression for the **Bethe free energy**:

$$
\boxed{\phi_B = \frac{\lambda}{4}m^2 - \frac{\lambda}{4}q^2 - \mathbb{E}\!\left[\log\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, \exp\!\left(\lambda m\, x_0 x_0^* - \frac{\lambda\rho}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q}\, x_0\, z\right)\right].}
$$

*(Here $m$ and $q$ appear in the prefactor with opposite sign and a different combination, $\frac{\lambda}{4}m^2 - \frac{\lambda}{4}q^2$, than the bare disorder-correction term $-\frac{\lambda}{4}\rho^2+\frac{\lambda}{4}q^2$ derived above; the extra $m^2$ piece arises from the analogous Stein's-lemma treatment of the planted cross term $\frac{\lambda}{n}x_0x_0^*\sum_i x_ix_i^*$, which was abbreviated in the cavity recursion but follows the identical computation.)*

This is a variational free energy in the same sense as the replica free energy of Lecture 1: extremizing over the order parameter $m$ reproduces the state-evolution fixed-point equation. Differentiating,

$$
\frac{\partial \phi_B}{\partial m} = 0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \lambda m - \lambda\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\, x_0 x_0^*\, \exp\!\big(\lambda m\, x_0 x_0^* - \frac{\lambda\rho}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q}\,x_0 z\big)}{\int dx_0\, P_X(x_0)\,\exp\!\big(\lambda m\, x_0 x_0^* - \frac{\lambda\rho}{2}x_0^2 + \sqrt{\lambda q}\,x_0 z\big)}\right] = 0,
$$

which, after canceling the overall factor of $\lambda$, is *exactly* the state-evolution fixed-point equation for $m$ derived via the cavity method earlier. This confirms that the cavity method's fixed point is a stationary point of $\phi_B$ — the Bethe free energy is the "potential" whose extremization the AMP iteration is implicitly performing, just as ordinary gradient descent extremizes a loss.

**Connection to the Franz–Parisi potential.** The function $\phi_B(m,q)$, viewed as a function of the order parameters before extremization, is closely related to the **Franz–Parisi potential** from the spin-glass literature: a free energy computed with one order parameter (here, the overlap with a reference configuration) held fixed, used to probe the structure of the energy landscape around a typical configuration — for instance, to detect the existence of multiple distinct minima (a signature of replica-symmetry breaking) even when the replica-symmetric free energy alone would not reveal it. The phase diagrams resulting from this analysis (regions of $(\alpha, \rho)$-space where Bayes-optimal reconstruction is information-theoretically possible, algorithmically achievable by AMP, or impossible) are developed further in the accompanying slides.
